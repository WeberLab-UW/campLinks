# Measuring Generative AI Use in Campaign Communications: Magnitude and Modality

We define two complementary measures to characterize generative AI use in campaign communications.

## Estimand

Because no detection tool operates with perfect sensitivity and specificity, all measures reported here estimate *detectable* AI-generated content rather than true AI-generated content. Given that PanGRAM's false negative rate is non-trivial, particularly for edited or hybrid human-AI text (Bao et al., 2024), our estimates should be interpreted as a lower bound on actual generative AI use in campaigns. The gap between detectable and actual use is itself a policy-relevant quantity, as it speaks directly to the feasibility of enforcement under current disclosure regimes.

## Magnitude

For a given candidate $c$ in election cycle $t$, let $D_{c,t} = \{d_1, d_2, \dots, d_n\}$ denote the set of content units (e.g., web pages, emails, social media posts) produced by the campaign. Each content unit $d_i$ has token length $w_i$ and is scored by a detection function $f(d_i) \in [0,1]$, representing the estimated probability that $d_i$ was generated by a large language model.

We report two variants of candidate-level magnitude. First, the mean detection probability, computed as a length-weighted average to ensure that substantive content (e.g., a detailed policy platform) contributes proportionally more than brief boilerplate (e.g., a donation page):

$$
M_{c,t}^{\text{prob}} = \frac{\sum_{d_i \in D_{c,t}} w_i \cdot f(d_i)}{\sum_{d_i \in D_{c,t}} w_i}
$$

Second, applying a classification threshold $\tau$, the length-weighted proportion of content classified as AI-generated:

$$
M_{c,t}^{\tau} = \frac{\sum_{d_i \in D_{c,t}} w_i \cdot \mathbb{1}[f(d_i) \geq \tau]}{\sum_{d_i \in D_{c,t}} w_i}
$$

where $\mathbb{1}[\cdot]$ is the indicator function. The continuous measure $M_{c,t}^{\text{prob}}$ captures gradations in detection confidence without dependence on a threshold choice, while $M_{c,t}^{\tau}$ provides a more interpretable proportion suitable for policy-relevant reporting. Because results may be sensitive to the choice of $\tau$, we report $M_{c,t}^{\tau}$ at multiple thresholds ($\tau \in \{0.5, 0.7, 0.9\}$) and present sensitivity curves showing how aggregate estimates vary across the full range of $\tau$.

### Candidate-Level Adoption Indicator

Because overall detection rates are low, we additionally define a candidate-level adoption indicator. Rather than flagging a campaign based on a single content unit exceeding $\tau$ (which would be vulnerable to false positives at scale), we require that at least $\kappa$ content units exceed the threshold:

$$
A_{c,t} = \mathbb{1}\left[\sum_{d_i \in D_{c,t}} \mathbb{1}[f(d_i) \geq \tau] \geq \kappa\right]
$$

Setting $\kappa = 1$ recovers the naive any-detection rule; higher values of $\kappa$ trade sensitivity for robustness to false positives. We calibrate $\kappa$ relative to PanGRAM's reported false positive rate $\alpha$ and the number of content units per candidate $|D_{c,t}|$, selecting $\kappa$ such that the probability of a false adoption flag under the null hypothesis of no AI use is held below a specified level $p^*$. Under the assumption that false positives occur independently at rate $\alpha$:

$$
P\left(\sum_{d_i \in D_{c,t}} \mathbb{1}[f(d_i) \geq \tau] \geq \kappa \ \Big|\ \text{no AI use}\right) = 1 - \sum_{j=0}^{\kappa-1} \binom{|D_{c,t}|}{j} \alpha^j (1-\alpha)^{|D_{c,t}|-j} \leq p^*
$$

This yields candidate-specific $\kappa$ values that account for the fact that campaigns with more content units face greater exposure to false positives.

### Aggregation

At the level of government $\ell$, the adoption rate is:

$$
R_{\ell,t} = \frac{1}{|C_{\ell,t}|} \sum_{c \in C_{\ell,t}} A_{c,t}
$$

where $C_{\ell,t}$ is the set of candidates at level $\ell$ in cycle $t$. We similarly aggregate both magnitude measures to the government level:

$$
\bar{M}_{\ell,t}^{\text{prob}} = \frac{1}{|C_{\ell,t}|} \sum_{c \in C_{\ell,t}} M_{c,t}^{\text{prob}} \qquad \bar{M}_{\ell,t}^{\tau} = \frac{1}{|C_{\ell,t}|} \sum_{c \in C_{\ell,t}} M_{c,t}^{\tau}
$$

## Modality

Campaign content spans multiple communication channels $k \in \mathcal{K}$ (e.g., website text, email, social media, images, video). For each modality $k$, we compute modality-specific versions of all three candidate-level measures -- $M_{c,t}^{\text{prob},(k)}$, $M_{c,t}^{\tau,(k)}$, and $A_{c,t}^{(k)}$ -- by restricting computation to content units of type $k$. The modality profile of a candidate is then the vector:

$$
\mathbf{m}_{c,t} = \left( M_{c,t}^{\text{prob},(k)},\ M_{c,t}^{\tau,(k)},\ A_{c,t}^{(k)} \right)_{k \in \mathcal{K}}
$$

This allows us to characterize not only how much AI-generated content a campaign produces, but where it concentrates its use. At the aggregate level, comparing modality-specific measures across levels of government and election cycles reveals systematic patterns in how campaigns adopt generative AI tools across different communication channels.
